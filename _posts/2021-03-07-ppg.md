	<div class="col-7"><b>“[It’s important] to get in with students and understand what they now know and can do and begin to chart out a path for individual students for how they can begin to recover whenever learning might have been lost during this period,”</b> said Chase Nordengren, a research scientist with the Portland, Ore.,-based education assessment group NWEA.
	<br><br> The amount of learning loss over the past year has not been as significant as researchers anticipated, according to Mr. Nordengren, who credits teachers for doing “extraordinary things with the tools and the circumstances they’ve been given.”
<br><br>But the closure of buildings affected more than student learning — it also took away their sense of normalcy, from losing contact with friends, to missing out on some of the resources provided by schools, such as books and extracurricular activities.  

<br><br>“We’ve noticed anecdotally, and I think empirically, some really hard impacts on a lot of kids,” Mr. Nordengren said. “So the most expediently we can help them get back to that sense of normal, of the day-to-day of schooling, the better.”
